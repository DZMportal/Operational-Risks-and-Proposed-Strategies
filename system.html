OPERATIONAL RISKS

RISKS ENCOUNTERED DURING TRANSPORTATION AT SEA

FAILURES IN CARGO SYSTEM

Maritime accidents involving cargo can occur at various stages of a vessel’s journey, including during loading and unloading operations at the port, throughout the voyage at sea, or upon arrival at the destination terminal. These incidents may lead to substantial financial and operational losses. For instance, one of the most hazardous activities in maritime logistics involves the use of cranes and hoisting equipment during port operations (Maritime Injury Guide, 2023). Malfunctions or failures in such equipment can disrupt cargo-handling processes, cause operational delays, increase repair costs, and pose significant safety risks to workers. The consequences of these failures extend beyond the immediate disruption, potentially damaging the operational integrity, financial stability, and overall reputation of the companies engaged in maritime cargo transport.

Cargo handling equipment (CHE) is a fundamental component of port operations, encompassing a range of machinery such as automated guided vehicles, cranes, and forklifts. These machines are designed to manage heavy loads, often under challenging environmental and operational conditions. However, without sufficient maintenance, the prolonged use of such equipment can lead to mechanical wear and tear, resulting in unexpected breakdowns. When this occurs, cargo handling processes may come to a standstill, causing severe backlogs and delays that are difficult to resolve promptly (Servi-Tek, 2021). These disruptions not only impair the efficiency of cargo movement but also have considerable financial implications. Missed deadlines and delays in delivery can lead to contractual disputes and strained relationships with clients.

Furthermore, the functionality of cargo handling equipment is not solely dependent on its mechanical integrity. Human error also plays a significant role in contributing to equipment-related failures. Both unsafe acts and unsafe conditions can cause accidents and injuries, but they need to be handled in different ways. Unsafe acts usually happen because of human error or risky behavior, so they’re best addressed through proper training and consistent enforcement of safety rules. On the other hand, unsafe conditions often involve problems with the physical environment, such as faulty equipment or poor lighting, and fixing them might mean making repairs, updating equipment, or changing the workspace to remove the hazard (HSS, 2024).
