OPERATIONAL RISKS

RISKS ENCOUNTERED DURING LOADING-UNLOADING OPERATIONS

BAD WEATHER CONDITIONS

Port operations are significantly impacted by severe weather occurrences, particularly when it comes to cargo loading and unloading. Approximately 72% of port authorities state that extreme weather substantially impacts their operations, according to a survey by the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD, 2023). Seventy-six percent report operational disturbances, while about 60% report delays in loading and unloading procedures. In addition, 45% report that severe weather physically damages infrastructure. These disruptions, in addition to being costly, undermine the efficiency of international supply networks and make ports more vulnerable to environmental conditions. This underlines the importance of rigorous contingency planning and stronger infrastructure. PortCalls Asia (2024) also reports that heavy rains during the monsoon season, especially during Tropical Storm Maring, seriously disrupt operations at Philippine ports. Flooded streets and highways make it difficult for trucks to reach the piers, slowing down the flow of cargo. Even though the ports remain open, the severe weather causes delays in unloading and transporting goods, highlighting how much bad weather can affect port loading and unloading operations. This situation shows how vulnerable the country’s port operations are to climate conditions, which can easily throw off schedules and reduce overall efficiency.

According to Putri et al. (2020), environmental factors like high noise levels and hot working conditions contribute to fatigue among port workers involved in loading and unloading operations. Their study shows that 71.2% of workers report experiencing significant tiredness, influenced by factors such as age, eating habits, especially whether they had breakfast, and the work environment. While the study doesn’t find a statistically strong link between heat and fatigue on its own, it points out that the combined effects of physical labor and environmental stressors negatively affect workers’ well-being and performance. This highlights the importance of considering high temperatures when looking for ways to improve conditions during loading and unloading operations.

The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) (n.d.) further emphasizes that over 90 percent of globally traded goods are transported by sea, and this figure is expected to triple by 2050. This underscores the critical importance of safeguarding maritime trade from disruptions caused by extreme weather. Containers account for about 60 percent of these shipments, carrying everything from electronics to food products, while bulk goods like crude oil and cereals are transported in large cargo holds. Given this immense volume of trade, even small disruptions in maritime operations can lead to widespread delays and inefficiencies.
