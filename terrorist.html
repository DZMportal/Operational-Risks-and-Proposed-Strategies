OPERATIONAL RISKS

RISKS ENCOUNTERED DURING TRANSPORTATION AT SEA

TERRORIST ACTIVITIES

According to Oreta (2023), maritime terrorism accounts for a small share of global terrorist activity, with most incidents being minor, aside from major events like the ferry bombings in the early 2000s. Yet, the use of maritime routes for terrorist fundraising and logistical operations remains a major concern. The persistence of smaller scale attacks, such as the nearly 200 actual and attempted armed assaults on vessels in Southeast Asia from 2014 to 2018, further illustrates the ongoing threat. Moreover, as highlighted by the World Ocean Review (n.d.), even isolated incidents can have wide ranging consequences, including disrupted supply chains, increased insurance costs, and heightened operational risks. Therefore, while shipping lines in this study may not frequently experience terrorist-related threats, the global implications of maritime terrorism justify continued vigilance and the implementation of strong security measures.

The significance of maritime security has long been recognized as integral to the global shipping industry. Historically, issues such as piracy, smuggling, and illegal fishing have posed challenges to safe maritime operations. Maritime terrorism is distinct in its potential to magnify consequences. For instance, an attack on a supertanker could result in widespread environmental disasters, such as oil spills, while also triggering economic instability in affected regions. According to UNISCO (n.d.), one of the issues related to terrorist activities is about the cargo screening process. One major issue is the high volume of cargo that must be inspected, often resulting in congestion and delays at ports.

Additionally, the process requires significant time, labor, and investment in modern technologies. Certain threats, such as explosives or narcotics, are particularly difficult to detect due to how well they can be concealed within shipments. These challenges are particularly relevant to terrorist activities, as undetected hazardous materials or weapons could be used for illicit purposes. Health and safety concerns, including potential radiation exposure from some screening technologies, add another layer of complexity. External disruptions such as adverse weather or equipment failure can also hinder operations, making it harder to ensure thorough security measures. Moreover, cargo screening depends on well-trained personnel capable of accurately interpreting images and data to identify potential risks, which is crucial for preventing terrorist-related incidents. These challenges emphasize the need for improved cargo inspection protocols to reduce vulnerabilities to terrorism in the maritime sector.
